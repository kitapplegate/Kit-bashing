title: 'Why the Jump'

---

I was at a job fair when it actually clicked. Not exactly the setting you
picture when someone talks about a life-altering revelation, but that's
where I was standing, so that's where it happened.

These days I work as an equipment consultant for a company that gets hired
out by insurance companies — something gets damaged, they send me out to
look at it and make a call: clean it, repair it, write it off. It's a fine
job. Nothing wrong with the company, nothing wrong with the work. It's just
not *me*, in the way a shirt can technically fit and still feel wrong every
time you put it on. I'd been quietly applying elsewhere for months and
collecting a tidy stack of silence and "we're going in a different
direction," which is corporate for "no," said as gently as possible.

At the fair, I ended up talking to two people pitching an IT training
route, sponsored by [DAV](https://www.dav.org/). I gave them my honest
pushback: "Man, I'm 49. I already had a career. I started this one and I
don't love it. I feel like I'm too late to switch again." They didn't miss
a beat — technology moves so fast, they said, that everybody's playing
catch-up all the time, especially now with AI blowing the doors off
everything. Nobody's "already ahead." You're either learning or you're not.
Cheap motivational-poster logic, and it worked on me anyway.

I walked the rest of that job fair and nothing else stuck. So I sat with
it. Twenty years in the Coast Guard, and no college degree to show a hiring
manager for it — and I'll admit that one stings. Twenty years of real
experience apparently weighs less than a four-year degree and zero
experience, on paper, which is a fun thing to learn about the job market at
49. But the more I turned it over, the more I kept landing in the same
place: *let's just do it.* Let's jump into IT.

It's not a decision that came out of nowhere, to be fair. I started college
in an MIS program before life rerouted me. During COVID I taught myself a
programming language for fun, built a Raspberry Pi cluster in my living
room because I wanted to know how clustering actually worked, and messed
around in Linux just to break things and fix them again — the classic
hobbyist's cycle of self-inflicted problems. A lot of my friends live in
this world, and every time I talk to them about it, something in me lights
up in a way the equipment-inspection reports do not.

Is it a questionable decision? Extremely. By the time I finish this program
and get my CCNA, I'll be standing on the doorstep of 50, walking into
entry-level rooms next to people half my age with a decade-plus runway
ahead of them and, let's be honest, a head start on the material. I'm doing
it anyway, because for the first time in a while, thinking about the work
itself feels *good* instead of just tolerable.

**Why networking, and why AI specifically:** I don't actually want to be
the guy running cable and racking switches in a server closet — nothing
against that work, it's just not the itch. I want the cloud side, the part
where you're working from a machine that can see the ones and zeros
actually moving and trace the whole path a packet takes, not just plug in
a box and walk away. And AI is the thing I genuinely can't put down. I'm
reading constantly, building constantly. I'd put myself past the "I know
how to type a good prompt into ChatGPT" stage and into actually wanting to
understand what's happening underneath — which, fittingly, is most of why
this blog exists in the first place.

...but in all seriousness — here's the real reason for the blog itself.
It isn't really for an audience. It's for my kids, and their kids, and
whoever comes after them. In my church we take journaling seriously —
recording your own history is part of the point, so the people who come
after you actually know who you were, not just a name on a family tree.
We're not in the era of somebody finding a leather diary in a drawer
anymore. If my grandkids or great-grandkids ever want to know what their
grandfather was doing with his one life, they're going to search for it
online. So that's what this is — a record, kept in public, of a guy who
spent twenty years handling hazmat and oil spills deciding, at 49, to
start completely over. If anyone else gets something out of following
along, great. But first and foremost, this one's for posterity.

Questionable decision. Full steam ahead.
